Flow Towers

The Foundation operates several stations of flows located in different ecosystems in Spain, which continuously measure the flows of carbon, water vapor and energy between the ecosystem and the atmosphere.

The data collected is used to study the carbon and water cycle of these Mediterranean ecosismteas, and their response to seasonal and interannual climate variability. The eddy covariance technique is a direct non-intrusive method, and currently one of the most defensible to measure turbulent vertical flows in the biosphere-atmosphere interface, at ecosystem scale (soil and vegetation). It has become a popular tool for determining water and carbon exchange rates over natural ecosystems, fields, and other soils or aquatic systems, and is also frequently used to measure the exchange of other trace gases such as methane or ozone.

However, this method is based on important assumptions and involves complex mathematical calculations, therefore, requires experience and care when selecting the experimental site, configuring instruments, processing the data by applying the appropriate corrections and evaluating the quality of the data. towers stations also perform routine measurements of a standard set of micrometeorological and environmental variables, including meteorological variables (incoming solar radiation, net radiation, air temperature and relative humidity, atmospheric pressure, precipitation, wind direction and velocity) and soil variables (soil temperature profile , soil moisture profile).

Depending of specific objectives, additional continuous measurements can be integrated to flux tower stations, such as proximal in-situ remote sensing (surface IR temperature, surface reflectance measurements for vegetation indexes), soil CO2 efflux chamber systems, sapflow sensors, lysimeters, others traces gases fluxes by eddy covariance, etc…

For most of the variables, the continuous measurements are automatically averaged and logged at 10-min and 30-min periods by data loggers or computers. Data are then transferred every day through ·g mobile connection form the experimental sites to the servers of Fundation CEAM to be stored and archived.

All these variables are then primarily used to calculate and analyze the carbon, water and energy budgets of the studied ecosystems, and characterize their variability at temporal scales from hours to decades. As global climate change is mostly driven by the increase of CO2 in the atmosphere, scientists are particularly interested in these measurements for robust quantification of current ecosystem services, such as carbon sequestration or hydrological fluxes regulation, predictions of futures changes and planning for mitigation and adaptation. Data from flux tower stations are also extensively used for tuning and validation of models (global climate models, mesoscale and weather models, complex biogeochemical and ecological models), as well as for calibration and validation of remote sensing products from satellites and aircraft.
